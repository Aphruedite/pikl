02 — Why I Build

I'm drawn to products that sit inside messy, high-stakes systems.

That instinct didn't come from a framework. It came from living in exactly those systems for most of my adult life.

The script I was handed

I grew up in Goa with a very clear script.

Good daughter, good student, good wife. At 23, I moved to the US after an arranged marriage — a dependent on someone else's visa, in someone else's life, in a country I was still learning to navigate. The marriage turned out to be abusive. The path that had been charted for me was not one I was going to stay on. So I changed the plan.

The moment things changed

Carnegie Mellon. Under 4% acceptance. Last round.

I studied nights while my husband slept. Then a false report from my ex triggered a university investigation. A minor irregularity in authorized on-campus work collapsed my immigration status overnight. Months of legal research, institutional dead ends, thousands of dollars. I eventually had to leave. I packed my life in two days.

I lived the exact problem Pikl solves. Not in theory — in a Pittsburgh apartment, at 2am, alone.
What I learned about products

I kept thinking about how avoidable so much of it had been.

Not the abuse. But the cascading failures — the confusion, the opacity, the absence of any trusted place to think clearly before making irreversible decisions. That's when I understood what kind of products I want to spend my life building. Not products that inform. Products that help people decide.

Why Pikl is the convergence

Pikl is the most personal version of everything I've built.

6+ years building AI-enabled products in analytics, education, compliance, and safety — all high-stakes, all built around users who couldn't afford to get it wrong. Pikl is where that experience and that pain arrive at the same place. I'm building the product I once needed. And I'm not going to stop until it exists.

05 — Build Log

Building Pikl means thinking out loud.

Apr 2026
Why I stopped calling it a platform
Every time I described Pikl as a platform, the idea got bigger and blurrier. A platform implies infrastructure. What I'm actually building is a moment — the moment before a student makes a decision they can't take back. So I stopped calling it a platform and started calling it a decision layer. That one word change made everything else clearer.
Mar 2026
Locking the three core flows
Job offer review. OPT timeline. DSO conversation prep. These are the three moments where international students are most alone and most likely to make an irreversible mistake. Everything else can come later. The MVP is the three flows done well.
Feb 2026
From pain to product: sharpening the positioning
Spent two weeks interviewing international students. The pattern is always the same: the information exists somewhere, but there's no trusted private space to apply it to their actual situation before acting. That's the gap. That's Pikl.
