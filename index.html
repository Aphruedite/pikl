Why I build

I’m drawn to products that sit inside messy, high-stakes systems.

That instinct didn’t come from a framework. It came from living in exactly those systems for most of my adult life.

The script I was handed

I grew up in Goa with a very clear script.

Good daughter, good student, good wife. At 23, I moved to the U.S. after an arranged marriage, a dependent on someone else’s visa, in someone else’s life, in a country I was still learning to navigate. The marriage turned out to be abusive. The path that had been charted for me was not one I was going to stay on. So I changed the plan.

The moment things changed

Carnegie Mellon. Under 4% acceptance. Last round.

I studied nights while my husband slept. Then a false report from my ex triggered a university investigation. A minor irregularity in authorized on-campus work collapsed my immigration status overnight. Months of legal research, institutional dead ends, thousands of dollars. I eventually had to leave. I packed my life in two days.

I lived the exact problem Pikl solves. Not in theory, in a Pittsburgh apartment at 2am, alone.
What I learned about products

I kept thinking about how avoidable so much of it had been.

Not the abuse. But the cascading failures: the confusion, the opacity, the absence of any trusted place to think clearly before making irreversible decisions. That’s when I understood what kind of products I want to spend my life building. Not products that inform. Products that help people decide.

Why Pikl is the convergence

Pikl is the most personal version of everything I’ve built.

Six-plus years building AI-enabled products in analytics, education, compliance, and safety, all high-stakes, all built around users who couldn’t afford to get it wrong. Pikl is where that experience and that pain arrive at the same place. I’m building the product I once needed. And I’m not going to stop until it exists.

The real situation

You’re making life-altering decisions with no good place to think clearly.

Pikl stays narrow on purpose. It starts in the moments where international students are most likely to make an irreversible mistake.

Reddit is fast and often wrong

One bad answer in a high-stakes moment is worse than no answer at all.

DSO office hours are slow

The MVP

Three questions every F-1 student dreads. Answered.

01

“Can I take this job or internship?”

You got an offer. You’re not sure if it’s safe, if you need CPT, or what needs to happen before you say yes. Pikl reviews the role, flags what’s missing, and helps you show up to the DSO conversation prepared instead of panicked.

02

“What do I need to do about OPT, and when?”

OPT timing is unforgiving. Miss a window and you lose months. Pikl turns your graduation date, offer status, and current stage into a real sequence with the decisions that matter and the deadlines you can’t afford to miss.

03

“Do I need to talk to my DSO, and how?”

When something feels off, students often either say too much or wait too long. Pikl helps you understand what you’re dealing with, what to bring, and how to open the conversation without making it harder than it needs to be.

Because you’ve been burned before

Your situation. Your data. Your call.

Student-owned by design

Everything you share with Pikl belongs to you. Not your school, not your employer, not anyone else.

Private by default

Pikl is built as a private layer for the student. The point is to help you think before you act, not to expose you while you’re figuring things out.

Not legal advice. Still useful.

Pikl helps you understand the situation, the gaps, and the next questions to ask. It helps you arrive prepared.

The goal

The student is never the least-prepared person in the room.

Pikl starts with the moment of decision: the job offer, the OPT deadline, the question you don’t know how to ask. Over time it can become the student-side operating layer for international students navigating work, compliance, and status across their time in the U.S.

Not to replace any institution. To make sure the student arrives ready.

Apr 2026

Why I stopped calling it a platform

Every time I described Pikl as a platform, the idea got bigger and blurrier. A platform implies infrastructure. What I’m actually building is a moment: the moment before a student makes a decision they can’t take back. So I stopped calling it a platform and started calling it a decision layer. That one word change made everything else clearer.

Mar 2026

Locking the three core flows

Job offer review. OPT timeline. DSO conversation prep. These are the three moments where international students are most alone and most likely to make an irreversible mistake. Everything else can come later. The MVP is the three flows done well.

Feb 2026

From pain to product: sharpening the positioning

The pattern is always the same: the information exists somewhere, but there’s no trusted private space to apply it to someone’s actual situation before they act. That’s the gap. That’s Pikl.
