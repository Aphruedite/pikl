Why I build

I am drawn to products that sit inside messy, high-stakes systems.

Not because the domains are dramatic. Because the design problem keeps repeating. People have information, fragments of policy, half-helpful guidance, and real consequences. What they usually do not have is a private, trustworthy layer that helps them reason through their exact situation before they act.

The instinct

I keep choosing problems where clarity changes outcomes.

That has shown up in healthcare, worker safety, and international student experience. Different users. Different domains. Same structural failure: the system makes sense to itself, but not to the person trying to move through it.

The pattern

Information existing is not the same thing as guidance landing.

I care less about content abundance than about decision quality. When the right next step is hard to see, people guess, wait too long, or escalate too late. The cost is never just confusion. It is what confusion turns into.

Why Pikl

This is the most personal version of the problem I have built around.

I lived the version of it that mattered most to me. That is why Pikl is narrow, student-first, and deliberately private. It is not trying to replace institutions. It is trying to make sure the student arrives ready.

International student experience · field research · service design

The time paradox

This project started as student housing research. It ended as a much sharper systems insight: the issue was not just access to housing. It was the absence of a trusted way to apply fragmented information in the right order, under time pressure.

4–6 weeks on roommate search 8–10 weeks total search 35% ideal combinations 8.5/10 stress
Why it matters

Not a housing problem. A sequencing problem.

What looked like a housing search was actually two intertwined searches: one for a compatible roommate and one for actual accommodation. Most students were prioritizing the first, even though the second was more time-sensitive. That misalignment produced avoidable compromises, rushed decisions, and a level of stress that was far higher than the system acknowledged.

“I spent so much time finding the perfect roommate match, only to realize we had just two weeks to find a place to live.” Sarah, graduate student from Australia
What changed

The insight was not “students need more information.”

The deeper pattern was that information already existed — through university boards, Facebook groups, WhatsApp threads, listings, and peer advice — but it did not assemble into a decision. That was the through-line that later became much more important to me.

Reframed the problem

Moved it from “housing search is hard” to “students are forced to make high-stakes decisions across fragmented channels, in the wrong order.”

Changed the unit of design

Designed around the moment of decision, not just the marketplace. The important question became: what should a student know, compare, and resolve before acting?

Made the later leap possible

This was the clearest early evidence that the missing product layer was private, contextual guidance before escalation — not just more content.

Lesson: people do not just need information. They need somewhere to think clearly with it before the stakes arrive.

Philips Respironics · AI strategy · regulatory engineering

The $664K paper trail

This project began with a broad prompt about generative AI and software process improvement. The real opportunity only appeared once the system was narrowed to one user living at the center of multiple handoffs: the regulatory engineer.

20+ interviews 16 opportunities identified 5 teams $664K annual cost
Why it matters

The structural failure was hiding inside documentation work.

The original brief was wide. Interviews across software development, V&V, design quality, and regulatory work revealed a much more focused opportunity: documentation creation for FDA submissions and design history files was pulling time, coordination, and cognitive load from multiple teams — but the burden became concentrated most visibly in regulatory engineering.

“Every submission is like telling a story to the regulators.” Regulatory engineer interview summary
What changed

From AI enthusiasm to one sharply framed bottleneck.

The strongest move in this project was not the final concept. It was the narrowing. Instead of treating “software process improvement” as the opportunity, the work converged on one role, one kind of work, and one measurable cost center.

Reframed the opportunity

Shifted from a broad AI exploration to a targeted question: how might generative AI optimize documentation creation for regulatory engineers in software development?

Made the cost legible

Quantified the process burden and turned a vague internal pain point into something the organization could reason about economically.

Designed for the handoff layer

The solution lived where teams, documents, and compliance logic met — not inside a generic AI layer floating above the work.

Lesson: when a system feels too broad to fix, find the person carrying the handoffs. That is usually where the product really begins.

MSA Safety · oil and gas field research · product opportunity

Beyond compliance

This project started with a brief about improving safety training efficiency and efficacy. Fieldwork pushed it somewhere more interesting: the real product problem sat in motivation, trust, behavior, and the poor way training efficacy was being measured on site.

Fatality rate 7x other industries Ground worker + safety manager Incidents, quizzes, informal evals Existing device data available
Why it matters

Training was easy on paper. The reality was different.

Across the research, the product challenge moved away from content delivery and toward lived site conditions: hazardous environments, worker turnover, conflicting priorities, repetitive internal training, confidence-driven shortcuts, and measurement systems that only caught failures after they happened.

The strongest framing shift in this project was simple: training efficacy was being assessed through incidents, near misses, quizzes, and informal observation — yet those methods were manual, incomplete, and often too late to change behavior upstream.

What changed

The product moved from training delivery to behavior change.

The client brief asked how to improve and monitor training efficiency and efficacy. The fieldwork made the better question visible: how might a continuous, measurable training experience address workers’ intrinsic motivations and behavioral deficiencies in the actual context of the work?

Shifted the core problem

Moved from “how do we provide training?” to “how do we influence behavior, trust, and follow-through on site?”

Reframed measurement

Identified that incidents, quizzes, and informal observation were insufficient proxies for whether training was actually landing before risk materialized.

Found a real product advantage

MSA already had relevant worker and equipment data. The opportunity was to use that infrastructure to make training responsive to site reality instead of periodic and static.

Lesson: in high-risk environments, the product is not the training module. It is the loop between behavior, trust, context, and what gets noticed in time.

Why the product is narrow

The point is not to become a giant platform too early. The point is to be genuinely useful in the moments before an international student makes a decision with immigration, timing, or institutional consequences attached.

private by design student-first founder-led
The real situation

You're making life-altering decisions with zero reliable info.

Reddit is fast and often wrong

One bad answer in a high-stakes moment is worse than no answer at all.

School portals are built for schools

Not for you, your situation, or your actual offer letter on the table.

DSO office hours are slow

You need clarity now. Waiting two weeks for an appointment does not work.

The first three flows

Three questions every F-1 student dreads. Answered.

01

Can I take this job or internship?

Pikl helps evaluate the role, flag what is missing, and prep the DSO conversation before the student says yes too quickly.

02

What do I need to do about OPT — and when?

Pikl turns dates, status, and current stage into an actual decision timeline instead of scattered reminders and vague policy pages.

03

Do I need to talk to my DSO — and how?

Pikl helps the student understand what is going on, what to bring, and how to frame the issue before escalating blindly.

Because students have been burned before

Your situation. Your data. Your call.

Student-owned by design

Everything shared with Pikl belongs to the student. Not the school. Not the employer. Not the institution being escalated to.

Private before institutional

The product exists for the moment before someone emails the DSO or misses a deadline. That means privacy is part of the UX, not a legal footer.

Narrow on purpose

Pikl is not trying to replace lawyers or universities. It is trying to make sure the student arrives better prepared than they would have otherwise.
