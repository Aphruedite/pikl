I build for the moment before the wrong decision.

The systems that matter most are rarely the easiest to navigate. They're usually the opposite — fragmented, slow, full of unclear rules, and quietly unforgiving when someone gets the next step wrong.

I've spent most of my adult life inside those systems. As a user, as a researcher, as a PM. And the pattern I keep seeing is the same: people who are smart and capable, doing their best with incomplete information, in a system that was never really designed with them in mind.

That's the problem I come back to. Not because it's interesting in the abstract — but because I know what it costs when it isn't solved. I build products for the moment before confusion becomes consequence. For the user who just needs the path to be clear long enough to take the next step.

02 — Why I Build

I'm drawn to products that sit where confusion and consequence meet.

Not as an intellectual exercise. As a pattern I keep returning to because I understand it from the inside.

Opening thesis

The systems that shape people's lives are rarely designed with the person navigating them in mind.

They're designed for institutions, for scale, for legal protection. The human on the other end is expected to figure it out. And most of the time, they do. Until they don't.

That gap — between what the system requires and what the person actually understands in the moment — is where I've spent my career. It's also where I've spent significant parts of my life. Those two things are not a coincidence.

Founder origin

I didn't choose this problem space. It chose me.

I grew up in Goa with a clear script: be the good daughter, marry well, build a quiet life. At 23, I moved to the United States after an arranged marriage. My American dream was quickly turning into a nightmare. The life that had been mapped out for me was not one I was going to stay on.

So I changed the plan. I applied to Carnegie Mellon's Master of Integrated Innovation program in the last round, with less than 4% acceptance. I got in. I moved to Pittsburgh. I started over — this time on my own terms.

At CMU, I did what a lot of international students do: I balanced academics, campus jobs, internships, and the constant low-grade pressure of trying to build a future in a country that doesn't make it easy. It was hard in the specific way that good hard things are hard. I was moving forward.

Then, just as things were stabilizing — degree finished, job lined up — a faculty member I worked for offered guidance on my employment that turned out to be wrong. What was meant to be well-intentioned aid became the bane of my existence in the United States. The irregularities it created triggered a university investigation. What followed was months of legal research, institutional dead ends, and thousands of dollars — and the particular kind of fear that only makes sense if you've had your immigration status pulled out from under you without warning.

I sat in my DSO's office and explained my circumstances carefully. What I got in return was a woman in pearls telling me to seek asylum or couch surf back in India. $150,000 spent in tuition, and the best the institution could offer was a hug and a sentence about empathy from someone who did not, and could not, understand what was actually at stake. I did not need empathy. I needed a path forward.

I eventually had to leave. I packed my life into two days and came home.

It has been exactly a year. I thought I could put it behind me. I cannot.

Every time I open Reddit I find myself in the r/F1 threads, and there it is — a student asking the same questions I was asking, in the same spiral I was in, with the same absence of anything useful to hold onto. The helplessness comes flooding back. And so does the clarity about why I'm here.

"I'm not telling this story for sympathy. I'm telling it because it is the precise origin of everything I'm building. Another student with real prospects and a real future should not have that taken away over something that could have been understood, navigated, and prevented — if only they'd had somewhere to go before it became irreversible."
What that taught me

What I learned wasn't about immigration. It was about systems.

When your status collapses, you go looking for information. You find plenty of it. Official pages, Reddit threads, WhatsApp groups, legal aid directories, DSO FAQs. The information exists. It's just almost completely useless when you're trying to apply it to your exact situation, under real time pressure, with real consequences attached.

That's when I understood something I'd been circling in my product work for years without fully naming it.

The problem is never just lack of information. The problem is the absence of a trusted place to apply information to a specific situation before you act on it.

People in high-stakes systems don't need more content. They need structured help at the moment of decision. Without that, they guess. They ask the wrong person. They wait too long. They act too fast. And the system, which was never designed to catch them, doesn't.

That pattern — smart, capable people failed by systems that weren't designed with them in mind — is the pattern I've been building against my entire career. In analytics. In education. In compliance. In safety. I just hadn't built it yet for the problem that taught me what it meant.

"The problem is never just lack of information. The problem is the absence of a trusted place to apply information to a specific situation before you act on it."
The convergence

Pikl is where my life and my work arrive at the same place.

I've spent six years building products in high-stakes domains. An AI analytics platform where 3,000+ users needed insight they could actually act on. A K-12 learning product where students dropped off in the first session because the system wasn't meeting them where they were. Compliance workflows where errors weren't just inefficient — they were costly and trust-eroding. Safety platforms where the margin for confusion was essentially zero.

In every case, the underlying challenge was the same: a system that worked in theory, and a user who needed it to work in practice, right now, in their specific circumstances.

Pikl is the most direct version of that problem I've ever worked on.

There are 1.1 million international students in the US right now. A significant share navigating the same fragility I was — work authorisation decisions, filing deadlines, moments where one wrong call can unravel years of effort. Nobody has built the private, student-first layer that sits between "I don't know what to do" and "I need a lawyer." That's the gap. That's what Pikl fills.

"Founder-market fit, to me, doesn't mean domain knowledge or empathy. It means understanding the problem at a level that can't be researched into — only lived through."
